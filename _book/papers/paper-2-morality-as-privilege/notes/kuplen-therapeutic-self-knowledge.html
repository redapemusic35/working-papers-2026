Self-Knowledge

  • To have self-knowledge is to have a clear sense of who “we truly are”.
    • Having a clear sense of who we are is necessary for leading a meaningful authentic life.

What does it mean to have a sense of who we are?

Having a clear sense of who we are is referenced by knowledge of our own mental states (experiences, thoughts, emotions, beliefs, desires, and other mental states) ‘that constitute the self’.

There are two main sources of self-knowledge.

1 First-Personal Perspectives (Introspection)

  • Self-Knowledge
    • Agency is defined as an aspect of first-person authority.
    • It is our acknowledgement that psychological states are our own and that we are privileged over our psychological states.
    • We can shape our psychological states in a way we find appropriate.
      • Moral deliberations, being able to deliberate on the appropriateness of our feelings
      • We feel rather than believe, that a given mental state is emotionally convincing and harmonizes with our ‘patterns of feelings, thoughts, and actions’.

2 Third-Personal Perspectives

  • Self-Perception

Importance and Worth

As we pointed out in the first section, our emotions reflect the sense of importance and worth we ascribe to something or someone rooted in the particular way we are as a person. (kupl20?)

  • Emotions == and reflect what we care about

  • Emotional responses to fiction are subject to same laws and so reflect what we care about [emotional realism, also see gilmore]. Our emotional engagement with fiction then depends on what we care about.

That is, we come to feel character’s fears, agonies, and alienations because we perceive (consciously or subconsciously) the portrayed situation relevant for our own well-being. (kupl20?)

For instance, Lazarus has argued “If their plight were not like our own, we would not react” [fill citation].

Narrative art then functionally simulates a given character’s experiences and actions. They help us to observe the characters’ experiences and grasp the meanings of the experiences. As such, narrative arts can also serve to make our own experiences intelligible to us. But according to Kuplen, it only does so when our ’own emotional appraisal fits with the character’s [65]. Kuplen argues that it is during this transaction where the individual exercises agency by assessing the appropriateness or inappropriateness of the emotional experience.

But as she has argued previously, our own knowledge of who we are comes from other similar sources, namely narratives in which we use narrative art to make sense of our experiences and by such processes, gradually discover who we are. Yet this requires that we are able to put ourselves ‘in the shoes’ of those characters.
