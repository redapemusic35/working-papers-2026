Two Classes of Evil in Medieval Bestiaries

  • Deceivers: Camouflage, imitation, seduction
    • Chameleon: camouflage
    • Manticore: accurate imitations
    • Sirens: seductive skills
  • Fear and Death Generators
  • Purpose: Educate illiterate via art for orthodoxy and proper behavior (Piñero Moral 2021)

Medieval taxonomy mirrors modern epistemic harms: misinformation, false narratives, bullshit.

Deception in Bestiaries → Modern Epistemic Harms

  • Medieval worries: Not just epistemic, but close to today
    • Misinformation (chameleon-like camouflage)
    • False/inauthentic narratives (manticore imitation)
    • Bullshit (siren seduction)

Beasts symbolize evil to promote right action.

Hermeneutic Injustice (Fricker 2007)

  • Definition: Gap in collective interpretive resources
    • Hinders making sense of one’s own experiences
  • Link to Deceptive Evil
    • Restricts hermeneutic participation in social life
    • Historically against women, but not exclusively
  • Manticore as artistic representation of this evil

Evil is “direct and intuitive by the victim” (Russell 2016, 1).

Hermeneutic injustice deceives like the manticore.

Origins of the Manticore

  • Persian literature (5th–4th BC, Ctesias)
    • Red skin, lion-sized, human-like face, 3 rows of teeth, scorpion tail
    • Eats men; imitates voices (Pliny)
  • Evolution:
Source Key Traits
Aristotle Rough as lion, grey eyes, sting darts spines
Pliny Fixed gaze, continuous teeth, imitates speech
Ctesias Azure eyes, blood color, flute/trumpet voice

Became symbol of evil, death, hell → Christ’s power over them.

Familiar vs. Unfamiliar Imagery

  • Familiar: Animals (lion, scorpion)
  • Unfamiliar: Human face, triple teeth → Immaterial evil
  • Why? Easier to intuit abstract concepts: pain, death, good vs. evil
  • Hermeneutic Tool: Confront evil’s attributes (seduces, deceives, harms)
